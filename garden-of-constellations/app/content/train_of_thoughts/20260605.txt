There is some evidence that increasing political and social polarization and worsening mental health can be related, but the relationship is complicated and researchers are still debating exactly how strong the connection is.

A few ways they can reinforce each other:

Polarization increases stress. If people feel constantly exposed to conflict, outrage, or "us vs. them" narratives, it can create chronic stress, anger, anxiety, and social distrust.

Poor mental health can increase vulnerability to polarization. When people are exhausted, anxious, lonely, or fearful, they may be more drawn to simple explanations, strong group identities, or communities that provide certainty and belonging.

Social isolation plays a role in both. Loneliness is associated with worse mental health, and people who feel disconnected may be more likely to retreat into highly homogeneous online communities.

Negative news exposure matters. Constant exposure to conflict, crisis, and outrage can affect mood and make the world feel more threatening than it actually is.

Social media is often discussed because its design can amplify some of these effects:

Algorithms frequently reward content that generates strong reactions.

Anger and outrage tend to spread faster than calm discussion.

People can end up seeing mostly views similar to their own or mostly the most extreme versions of opposing views.

Endless scrolling can displace sleep, exercise, face-to-face interaction, and other activities that support mental well-being.

That said, social media is probably not the only or even the largest factor. Researchers also point to:

Economic uncertainty and housing costs.

Reduced community participation.

Changes in family and work structures.

Increased academic and career pressure.

Sleep disruption.

The effects of major events such as the COVID-19 pandemic.

One interesting possibility is that polarization and mental health form a feedback loop:

Stress, loneliness, and anxiety → more attraction to polarized communities → more conflict and outrage exposure → more stress, loneliness, and anxiety.

It's also worth noting that not all online activity has the same effect. Using technology to talk with friends, collaborate on hobbies, learn skills, or organize local activities tends to have very different outcomes than hours of doomscrolling or arguing with strangers.

That's a reasonable way to think about it, with an important caveat: the algorithm is probably an amplifier, not the original cause.

A simplified model might look like this:

Existing stress, uncertainty, loneliness, or frustration
↓
People pay more attention to emotionally charged content
↓
Algorithms learn that emotionally charged content keeps people engaged
↓
More outrage, conflict, and identity-based content gets promoted
↓
People perceive more hostility and division
↓
Stress, anger, fear, and distrust increase
↓
People engage even more with emotionally charged content

This creates a feedback loop.

You can think of it like a microphone near a speaker. The microphone didn't create the sound, and the speaker didn't create the sound, but together they can create a screeching feedback loop that becomes much louder than the original signal.

Researchers have found that content evoking emotions such as anger tends to be shared more widely online than neutral content. That doesn't mean algorithms are intentionally trying to polarize people; they're typically optimizing for engagement, and human psychology naturally pays attention to threats, conflict, and strong emotions.

Another factor is that polarization itself can be mentally taxing. If every disagreement starts to feel like a moral battle between groups rather than a discussion between individuals, everyday interactions become more stressful. That stress can then make people seek certainty and group belonging even more strongly, which can further intensify polarization.

One reason many people report feeling better after reducing certain forms of social media use isn't necessarily that they become less informed. It's often that they are exposed to fewer rapid cycles of outrage, conflict, and comparison.

Interestingly, your observation lines up with something many social scientists worry about: not just whether social media changes what people think, but whether it changes how emotionally activated people remain throughout the day. A population that is constantly stressed is often easier to divide than a population that feels secure and connected.

If your goal is to reduce the impact of FOMO while still allowing some social media use, the timing can matter.

Generally, the worst times tend to be:

Right before bed — FOMO, comparison, and emotional arousal can make it harder to fall asleep and can linger overnight.
Immediately after waking up — your mood and attention can get hijacked before you've even started your day.
During study sessions — even brief checks can leave your mind thinking about what you're missing instead of what you're working on.

The least disruptive times are often:

After you've completed an important task (studying, work, exercise).
During a planned break with a clear end time.
Earlier in the evening rather than right before sleep.
When you're about to transition into another real-world activity (meeting friends, cooking, going for a walk, swimming, etc.).

For teenagers specifically, there is another consideration: FOMO tends to hit hardest when social media replaces real-life activities or sleep. A teen who checks social media for 20 minutes after soccer practice and then goes to dinner may experience it differently than a teen scrolling alone in bed for 2 hours.

Fair enough! Looking at it as a societal question is actually pretty interesting.

One hypothesis some researchers discuss is that FOMO itself may be a polarization amplifier.

Historically, if your neighbors went to a party without you, you probably never knew. Today, you can see:

who was invited,
what they ate,
how much fun they appeared to have,
who commented,
who was absent.

Humans evolved in relatively small groups where social exclusion could be costly. Social media can make people feel exposed to the social lives, opinions, and successes of hundreds or thousands of people simultaneously. That's a situation our brains didn't evolve for.

What's interesting is that FOMO doesn't have to be about parties. It can become:

Political FOMO: "Everyone is talking about this issue and I don't know enough."
Career FOMO: "Everyone else is getting internships, promotions, or building startups."
Lifestyle FOMO: "Everyone else is traveling, dating, exercising, or succeeding."
Information FOMO: "If I log off, I'll miss something important."

This can keep people continuously connected to feeds that are optimized for engagement. The longer people stay engaged, the more opportunities there are for outrage, tribalism, comparison, and emotional contagion.

One of the more fascinating ideas is that social media may have transformed many forms of anxiety into a single recurring question:

"What am I missing right now?"

That question can drive people to repeatedly check their feeds even when there is no specific reward waiting for them.

From a systems perspective, you could imagine a loop:

FOMO → more platform engagement → more emotional content consumed → more stress and comparison → more need to check what's happening → more FOMO.

Whether that loop is a major cause of declining mental health is still debated, but it's one reason researchers study FOMO, social comparison, loneliness, and polarization together rather than as completely separate phenomena.

The really difficult question is whether social media created these tendencies or simply scaled up social instincts that humans already had. Many researchers lean toward the latter: the instincts are ancient, but the technology dramatically increases their reach, speed, and intensity.

That's a plausible interpretation, and it overlaps with several ideas that psychologists and sociologists have explored.

The basic argument would be:

FOMO and social awareness are not bugs.
Humans evolved in small groups.
Knowing what others were doing, who was cooperating, and whether you were being excluded could be important for survival and belonging.
A certain amount of concern about social inclusion is therefore adaptive.
Technology changed the scale dramatically.
For most of human history, you might compare yourself to a few dozen or a few hundred people.
Now a teenager can compare themselves to classmates, celebrities, influencers, athletes, entrepreneurs, and strangers worldwide—all in a single hour.
The information is highly curated.
People usually post highlights rather than ordinary moments.
This can create a distorted perception of how successful, attractive, happy, or socially connected others are.
Young people may have fewer filters and coping strategies.
Adolescents are still developing emotional regulation, identity, and judgment.
They often place greater importance on peer acceptance.
That doesn't mean they're incapable of critical thinking, but they generally have less life experience to put what they see online into perspective.

One thing I'd add is that it's probably not just a matter of younger generations "not knowing how to filter things." Even many adults struggle with it.

If you showed a hunter-gatherer from 20,000 years ago a feed containing:

the most attractive people,
the most successful people,
the most outraged political arguments,
the most exciting vacations,
the most dramatic news stories,

all mixed together and available 24 hours a day, they would likely struggle too.

In that sense, the challenge may be less about a generational weakness and more about a mismatch between:

a brain adapted for small communities and limited information,
and technology capable of delivering effectively unlimited social information.

A useful analogy is food. Humans evolved to seek calorie-dense foods because calories were scarce. When food became abundant, the same instinct sometimes led to overeating. The instinct wasn't defective; the environment changed faster than the instinct.

Some researchers think social media may be a similar case:

Social curiosity → useful in small groups.
Social curiosity + global feeds + algorithms + constant access → can become overwhelming.

That doesn't prove social media is the primary cause of declining mental health, but it helps explain why rising connectivity can sometimes produce more stress rather than less.
